### Why This Matters to Surgeons

Every day, surgeons caring for elderly patients with hepatobiliary cancer face the same dilemma: when both approaches are anatomically feasible, should we prioritize parenchymal-sparing segmental/wedge resection or proceed with major hepatectomy? Major resection may offer superior oncologic clearance but carries substantially higher perioperative morbidity and mortality in older patients — prior NSQIP data demonstrate severe complication-associated mortality of 20.1% in elderly vs 10.8% in younger patients.[28] Current guidelines lack evidence specific to this growing population.

Our study of **76,110 elderly patients** from the SEER database addresses a distinct question from prior SEER analyses: whereas earlier studies examined **whether** elderly patients should undergo surgery (Zhang 2020, Front Oncol; Xia 2025, Medicine), we examine **which** surgery they should receive — providing three clinically actionable findings:

1. **Surgical resection confers a large and robust survival benefit** (PSM-adjusted HR 0.35; E-value 5.2). An unmeasured confounder would need a risk ratio >5.2 to explain this association — exceeding most clinically plausible variables, confirming that surgery is not merely selecting healthier patients.

2. **Major hepatectomy did not confer additional cancer-specific survival benefit over segmental resection** across all age bands ≥70 and both HCC and ICC (major HR 0.24; segmental HR 0.23). We transparently report that this comparison is sensitive to unmeasured confounding (E-value ≈ 1.3), and do not claim equivalence. However, the consistency of the finding across all elderly subgroups suggests that tumor anatomy and patient comorbidity should guide resection extent rather than a default preference for radical resection.

3. **A clinical risk score (HBI) identifies patients across a broad prognostic spectrum**, potentially aiding surgical decision-making regarding the appropriateness and extent of resection in elderly patients.

### Methodological Rigor

This study is, to our knowledge, the first to systematically compare segmental versus major hepatectomy across both HCC and ICC in elderly patients using formal causal sensitivity analysis. Beyond standard multivariable adjustment and propensity score matching, we applied E-value analysis — recommended by JAMA Network journals for observational studies — to quantify robustness to unmeasured confounding. We designed the study following the target trial emulation framework recently endorsed by JAMA,[22] with explicitly defined eligibility criteria, treatment strategies, and causal contrast. We further developed and externally validated a 4-model machine learning ensemble on TCGA-LIHC and ICGC-LIRI-JP, providing the first benchmark for SEER-trained hepatobiliary survival models. All sensitivity analyses (competing risk, leave-one-out exclusion, sequential model specification, frailty stratification) consistently supported the primary findings.

### Why JAMA Surgery

This work addresses a question that directly affects the surgical management of elderly patients — a demographic that will constitute an increasing proportion of the hepatobiliary surgical caseload. JAMA Surgery's readership of academic surgeons, hepatobiliary specialists, and surgical outcomes researchers is the ideal audience for evidence that informs the choice between parenchymal-sparing and radical resection. We believe the combination of clinical relevance, population-based scope, and rigorous causal sensitivity analysis aligns with the journal's standards.
