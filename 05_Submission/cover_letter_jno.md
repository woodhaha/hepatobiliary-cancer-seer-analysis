We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Major Hepatectomy vs Segmental Resection in Elderly Patients With Hepatobiliary Cancer"** for consideration as an Original Investigation in JAMA Network Open.

### Why This Matters

The choice between parenchymal-sparing segmental resection and major hepatectomy in elderly patients with hepatobiliary cancer is a common clinical dilemma that directly affects surgical decision-making in a rapidly aging population. Prior population-based studies have examined **whether** elderly patients should undergo surgery; our study addresses the distinct and clinically more relevant question of **which** surgery they should receive.

### Key Findings

In an analysis of **76,110 elderly patients** (age ≥65 years) from the SEER 18-registry database (2004–2022), we found:

1. **Major hepatectomy was not associated with improved cancer-specific survival** compared with segmental/wedge resection across all age bands ≥70 years and both HCC and ICC (HR 0.24 vs 0.23), though findings are sensitive to unmeasured confounding (E-value ≈ 1.3).

2. **Surgical resection conferred a large and robust survival benefit overall** (PSM-adjusted HR 0.35; E-value 5.2), extending prior work with formal causal sensitivity analysis.

3. **A 4-model survival ensemble** (Cox, RSF, XGBoost, DeepSurv) achieved C-index 0.756; external validation in TCGA-LIHC and ICGC-LIRI-JP demonstrated moderate discrimination with transparent discussion of transportability limitations.

### Methodological Strengths

Following the target trial emulation framework recently endorsed by JAMA (Cashin et al. 2025), we applied E-value analysis for unmeasured confounding, propensity score matching, competing risk analysis, and multiple sensitivity analyses including Geriatric Risk Index stratification and cancer-type stratified models. All code is publicly available.

### Fit With JAMA Network Open

JAMA Network Open's broad clinical readership and receptiveness to rigorous population-based observational studies make it an ideal venue for this work. The study addresses a question that affects a large and growing patient population — elderly patients with hepatobiliary cancer — and provides evidence to inform the risk-benefit trade-off between parenchymal-sparing and major resection.
